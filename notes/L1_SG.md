### **Study Guide: Lecture 1 - Introduction to NLP**

**1. Core Definition of NLP**

- Natural language processing (NLP) is an interdisciplinary field combining computer science, artificial intelligence, and computational linguistics.
- Its primary concern is the interaction between computers and human (natural) languages.
- The ultimate goal is to use automated, AI-driven techniques to allow computers to understand human language instructions and perform tasks.

**2. Primary Challenges in NLP (Highly Testable)** _Midterm Tip: Understanding why NLP is difficult is a classic exam topic. Be prepared to identify which specific challenge is represented by a given example sentence._

- **Ambiguity:** Human language requires context and common sense to resolve multiple potential meanings. You should be able to identify these subtypes:
    - **Word Sense / Meaning Ambiguity:** When a single word has multiple meanings. For example, "I saw her duck" (is "duck" an animal or an action of hiding?).
    - **Prepositional Phrase (PP) Attachment Ambiguity:** When it is unclear which part of the sentence a prepositional phrase modifies. For example, "I saw her duck with a telescope" (Who has the telescope?).
    - **Syntactic/Headline Ambiguity:** Poor phrasing that creates multiple violent or confusing interpretations. Examples include "San Jose cops kill man with knife", "Local High School Dropouts Cut in Half", or "Include your children when baking cookies".
    - **Pronoun Reference Ambiguity:** When it is unclear to whom or what a pronoun refers. For example, "The doctor often brings his dog to visits with patients, he just loves to give a big sloppy kiss" (Does "he" refer to the dog or the doctor?).
- **Language is Not Static:** Language is dynamic and constantly evolving over different generations and regions. Models must constantly adapt to new cyber lingo, slang, and shorthand (e.g., LOL, G2G, BFN, FWIW).
- **Scalability:** Modern NLP requires processing data at a massive, computationally expensive scale. Training corpora sizes vary drastically, from the Penn Treebank (~1 million words) to Wikipedia (2.9 billion words) and the broader web (several billions of words).
- **Hallucination in Generation:** A critical issue with modern Large Language Models (LLMs) where models learn to generate plausible-sounding text but fail to verify factual accuracy. Examples include models making up fake biographies for professors or failing at basic visual-reasoning tasks like counting the correct number of baseballs in an image.

**3. Key NLP Tasks and Applications** _Midterm Tip: Familiarize yourself with what these tasks achieve, as questions may ask you to map a real-world problem to the correct NLP task._

- **Machine Translation:** Automatically translating text from one language to another (e.g., Facebook translation tools).
- **Sentiment / Opinion Analysis:** Analyzing text to determine the underlying sentiment (positive, negative, or neutral), often used for market research or tracking brand opinions on Twitter.
- **Information Extraction:** Converting unstructured text (like news articles) into structured database entries to extract entities and their relationships.
- **Question Answering:** Systems designed to interpret user queries and retrieve or generate the correct answer (e.g., IBM Watson winning Jeopardy or Siri).
- **Advanced Modern Applications:** Thanks to LLMs, new applications include Math/Geometry problem solving at the Olympiad level (e.g., AlphaGeometry), Data Science Agents that can write code to analyze databases, and Embodied 3D Agentic AI (e.g., a robot navigating a room to prepare a first-aid station).

**4. The Shift to Modern NLP**

- **Large Language Models (LLMs):** Computational models trained on a vast amount of data, specifically designed for natural language processing and generation.
- They represent a paradigm shift where a single large model, provided with enough compute and data, can solve traditional tasks that used to require highly specialized, individual models.
